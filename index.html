Chapter 1The State

Establishes Fiji's constitutional identity, sovereignty, and the fundamental values the State is committed to upholding.

"Fiji is a democratic, sovereign State. Sovereignty belongs to the people of Fiji from whom all governmental authority is derived."
FPR Analysis
Section 1 establishes popular sovereignty — the principle that governmental authority derives from the people. This is formally uncontested but its practical meaning is complicated throughout the Constitution. The 2006 coup suspended popular sovereignty by force. Section 131, which gives the RFMF a mandate to protect Fiji's "well-being," creates a structural tension with s.1 that the CRC must resolve. A constitution that declares sovereignty belongs to the people while simultaneously giving the military a supervisory role over democratic governance cannot fully honour that declaration.
SovereigntyDemocracyFoundational
"This Constitution is the supreme law of the Republic of Fiji. Any law inconsistent with this Constitution is invalid to the extent of the inconsistency."
FPR Analysis
Section 2 declares constitutional supremacy but its practical force is undermined by the immunity provisions in Chapter 10, which shield those who overthrew the previous constitutional order. The tension between formal supremacy and embedded immunity is the 2013 Constitution's most fundamental internal contradiction. No constitution that protects those who abolished its predecessor can fully claim the legitimacy that supremacy implies. The Supreme Court's August 2025 advisory opinion reinforced s.2 by clarifying that constitutional amendment — not military action — is the legitimate mechanism for constitutional change.
SupremacyRule of lawImmunity tension
CRC Watch
The Commission must address whether the immunity provisions in Chapter 10 are compatible with genuine constitutional supremacy. This is the fundamental structural contradiction the review must confront.
"Citizenship of Fiji may be acquired by birth or by descent or by registration or naturalisation."
FPR Analysis
Section 3 establishes the basis for Fijian citizenship. Unlike earlier constitutions that distinguished between citizenship categories along ethnic lines, the 2013 Constitution treats citizenship as a single civic status. The section is stable and uncontested. Its practical significance lies in its interaction with the electoral provisions — all citizens over 18 have the right to vote, regardless of ethnic background, reinforcing the non-communal electoral framework.
CitizenshipCivic identityNon-communal
"Fiji is a secular State and there is freedom of religion in Fiji."
FPR Analysis
Section 4 explicitly establishes Fiji as a secular state — a significant departure from proposals in the post-1987 period to establish Christianity as a state religion. The 2013 Constitution's secularism was a deliberate design choice that recognised Fiji's religious diversity, particularly the significant Hindu and Muslim communities among Fijians of Indian descent. The provision interacts with the equality provisions in s.26, particularly regarding the GCC proposal to remove gender and sexual orientation protections — a proposal that is partly grounded in religious arguments.
Secular stateReligious freedomDiversity
"The Republic of Fiji is founded on the values of civic virtue and national unity, including respect for others and the rule of law; human dignity, human rights and human security; human flourishing, including good health, education and economic well-being; and freedom, equality and social justice."
FPR Analysis
Section 6 provides the foundational values framework. The inclusion of "human security" and "human flourishing" alongside traditional human rights language signals a more expansive conception of state obligations than earlier constitutions. The CRC must consider whether the governance failures identified in Fiji's post-2013 period — the FICAC investigations, parliamentary salary controversies, suppression of the FELRC report — are consistent with the values of "civic virtue," "human dignity" and "rule of law" that s.6 declares foundational.
Foundational valuesHuman dignitySocial justice
CRC Watch
The Commission should consider whether s.6 adequately guides interpretation of the Constitution's other provisions — and whether the institutional mechanisms exist to give effect to these values in practice.
Chapter 2Bill of Rights

Fiji's Bill of Rights is among the most comprehensive in the Pacific — including civil, political, economic, social and cultural rights. Several provisions are contested before the CRC.

"Every person has the right to life. A person must not be arbitrarily deprived of life."
FPR Analysis
Section 8 establishes the right to life. Fiji abolished the death penalty in 1999 and the 2013 Constitution entrenches its prohibition. The provision is formally stable but its practical force depends on the accountability mechanisms that give it teeth — particularly regarding deaths in custody. The death of Jone Vakarisi in military custody in 2026, with three RFMF personnel under investigation, raised direct questions about whether s.8 is given effective protection by the institutions responsible for enforcing it.
Right to lifeDeath penalty abolishedCustody accountability
"A person must not be subjected to torture of any kind, whether physical, mental or emotional, or to cruel, inhumane, degrading or disproportionately severe treatment or punishment."
FPR Analysis
Section 9 prohibits torture in absolute terms — one of the few rights in the Constitution that cannot be limited by law. Fiji is a signatory to the UN Convention Against Torture. The provision's practical enforcement depends on functional accountability mechanisms including the Human Rights Commission and the courts. The immunity provisions in Chapter 10 complicate accountability for alleged torture during the 2006–2013 period.
Absolute rightUNCATNon-derogable
"Every person has the right to personal liberty and must not be arrested or detained, except as permitted by law."
FPR Analysis
Section 11 protects personal liberty. Its practical application has been tested by the FICAC's arrest of journalist Charlie Charters — his lawyers alleged the arrest was conducted to pressure him into revealing confidential sources rather than as a genuine criminal investigation. Whether the arrest was consistent with s.11 remains a live legal question. The provision requires that detention be "as permitted by law" — which means the quality and independence of Fiji's courts determines how effectively s.11 is protected in practice.

"Every person charged with an offence has the right to be presumed innocent until proved guilty according to law."
FPR Analysis
Section 14 establishes the presumption of innocence — a foundational principle of criminal justice. In Fiji's current political context, its practical application to prominent cases — including FICAC charges against former Deputy Prime Ministers Biman Prasad and Manoa Kamikamica — raises questions about due process, the independence of FICAC, and whether the anti-corruption framework serves accountability or political purposes. The CRC should consider whether the current structure of FICAC — appointed by and reporting to the executive — adequately protects the independence that s.14 presupposes.
Presumption of innocenceFair trialFICAC independence
"Every person has the right to freedom of expression including the freedom to seek, receive and impart information and ideas; freedom of the press and other media; and freedom of imagination and creativity."
FPR Analysis
Section 17 guarantees freedom of expression but s.17(3) permits Parliament to restrict it through legislation in prescribed circumstances. This has enabled regulatory frameworks — including the Media Industry Development Authority under Bainimarama — that critics argue suppress rather than regulate expression. The National Referendum Bill 2025 further restricts communication during referendum campaigns, raising direct questions about whether the government's interpretation of the limitations on s.17 is consistent with the provision's protective intent. The arrest of journalist Charlie Charters under FICAC powers tested the practical limits of media freedom under s.17.

CRC Watch
The Commission should examine whether the limitations framework in s.17 has been used to suppress rather than regulate expression — and whether the Referendum Bill's communication restrictions are compatible with the right it purports to protect.
"Every person has the right to a free education in a public school from Year 1 to Year 13."
FPR Analysis
Section 22 establishes a constitutionally guaranteed right to free education from Year 1 to Year 13 — one of the most concrete socioeconomic rights in the Constitution. The coalition government has made commitments toward early childhood education. The CRC's review should consider whether this provision is adequately resourced in practice and whether its implementation meets the standard the Constitution sets. A constitutional right without adequate funding mechanisms is aspirational rather than enforceable.

"Every person is equal before the law and has the right to equal treatment and protection under the law including protection from discrimination on grounds of race, culture, ethnicity, colour, place of origin, sex, gender, sexual orientation, gender identity and expression, disability, age or religion."
FPR Analysis
Section 26 is among the most progressive equality provisions in any Pacific constitution — explicitly protecting sexual orientation and gender identity. No other Pacific Island state constitution contains equivalent protections. The Great Council of Chiefs submitted proposals to remove gender and sexual orientation from s.26(3). If accepted, Fiji would become the first Pacific nation to constitutionally roll back LGBTQ+ protections — a development that would significantly damage Fiji's relationship with development partners and undermine its international human rights commitments. The Commission's handling of this submission will define its legacy in the Pacific region.
EqualityLGBTQ+ rightsGCC proposalPacific context
CRC Watch
The GCC's proposal to remove sexual orientation and gender identity from s.26(3) is the most politically charged submission before the Commission. Any amendment would require a three-quarters parliamentary supermajority and referendum approval — which means it would need cross-party support including from NFP, whose constituency includes communities with strong views on equality.
"Every child has the right to be registered at birth; to a name; to a nationality; to basic nutrition, shelter, basic health care services and social services; to be protected from abuse, neglect, harmful cultural practices and exploitation including sexual exploitation."
FPR Analysis
Section 27 provides extensive rights protections for children — among the most comprehensive in the Pacific. The provision is formally stable and uncontested. Its practical implementation depends on resourcing social services and the functioning of the family court and child protection systems. The reference to protection from "harmful cultural practices" is significant in the Fijian context — it provides a constitutional basis for legislation addressing practices that disproportionately harm girls and women in some communities.
Children's rightsHarmful practicesBest interests
"Every mineral and mineral oil in or under Fiji or in or under the territorial waters of Fiji is owned by the State."
FPR Analysis
Section 30 vests all mineral ownership in the State. The Chair of the iTaukei Lands and Fisheries Commission, Ratu Meli Saubulinayau, has submitted to the CRC that this provision should be amended to recognise iTaukei ownership, arguing it represents a colonial legacy — before 1874 all resources were under iTaukei ownership. The submission goes to the heart of one of the 2013 Constitution's most debated provisions. The Vuda incinerator debate — in which the proposed site involves iTaukei customary land and the plant would be adjacent to the Dreketi Inlet — illustrates the real-world stakes of natural resource ownership questions under s.30.

CRC Watch
Any amendment to s.30 requires careful analysis of implications for Fiji's fiscal framework, existing royalty arrangements, and investor confidence. The Commission must engage seriously with this submission without allowing it to crowd out the broader reform agenda.
"A right or freedom set out in this Chapter may be limited by a law or by an act done under the authority of a law to the extent that the limitation is reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society based on human dignity, equality and freedom."
FPR Analysis
Section 45 provides the limitations clause — the provision that determines when rights may be restricted. The "reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society" standard is the same test used in South African and Canadian constitutional law. Whether Fiji's courts have applied this standard rigorously — or whether they have deferred to parliamentary judgment — significantly affects how protected the Bill of Rights actually is in practice. The National Referendum Bill's communication restrictions must be assessed against this standard.
Limitations clauseProportionalityDemocratic standard
CRC Watch
The Commission should consider whether the s.45 limitations standard is adequately applied by courts and whether there is a case for strengthening the proportionality requirements to prevent broad legislative overreach.
Chapter 3Recognition of iTaukei, Rotuman and Banaban Interests

Protects the land, fisheries and interests of Fiji's indigenous communities — among the most constitutionally entrenched provisions.

"The ownership of iTaukei land is protected and guaranteed. iTaukei land shall not be permanently alienated, whether by sale, grant, transfer or exchange."
FPR Analysis
Section 28 provides constitutional protection for iTaukei land — one of the most fundamental provisions in Fiji's constitutional history. Approximately 87 per cent of Fiji's land is iTaukei land, managed by the iTaukei Land Trust Board under the iTaukei Land Trust Act. The prohibition on permanent alienation reflects the primacy of customary land rights. The Vuda incinerator case illustrates how s.28 operates in practice — the proposed site is on land belonging to the Yavusa Koi Vuda of Lauwaki, and under established law any lease without TLTB approval is null and void. The EIA proceeding without secured iTaukei consent raised direct questions about whether regulatory processes adequately operationalise s.28's protection.
iTaukei landPermanent alienation prohibitedTLTB87% of land
"The ownership of iTaukei fisheries is protected and guaranteed and shall not be permanently alienated."
FPR Analysis
Section 31 protects iTaukei fishing rights — a provision of increasing importance as climate change threatens reef ecosystems and commercial fishing pressures increase. The provision interacts with the Waigani Convention questions raised by the Vuda incinerator proposal — a facility that would process foreign waste adjacent to iTaukei fishing grounds protected under s.31. The constitutional protection of fisheries is formally stable but practically dependent on environmental governance that protects the ecosystems on which those fisheries depend.

Chapter 4Parliament

Establishes Fiji's unicameral Parliament, the electoral system, and the rules governing legislative activity. Several provisions — particularly regarding party discipline and the electoral system — are under active CRC review.

"Members of Parliament are to be elected under a system of proportional representation in which each registered voter votes for a candidate in the election, and may cast a preferential vote for one or more further candidates; and the results of the election are determined primarily by the proportion of votes cast for the political parties."
FPR Analysis
Section 51 establishes the open list proportional representation system — the most significant structural change to Fiji's electoral architecture since independence. The elimination of communal rolls was intended to produce non-ethnic voting. However, the FELRC found that the open list system has created practical problems: ballot papers listing hundreds of candidates by number only, which voters found confusing; disproportional outcomes where candidates with thousands of votes were excluded while those with fewer votes entered Parliament; and a system that incentivises ethnic community outbidding rather than discouraging it. The FELRC recommended a Preferential Open List Constituency System (POLCS) as an alternative. The CRC cannot credibly review s.51 without access to the FELRC's full report.
Proportional representationOpen listFELRCElectoral reform
CRC Watch
The FELRC recommended changes to the electoral system after nationwide consultations and comprehensive analysis. The CRC cannot adequately review s.51 without the FELRC report — which has been with the Acting AG since July 2025 and has not been released. The Commission should formally request the report from the Acting AG before finalising any electoral system recommendations.
"Every citizen of Fiji who is at least 18 years old has the right to vote in elections for Parliament and to be elected to Parliament."
FPR Analysis
Section 53 establishes universal non-communal suffrage — eliminating the communal rolls that defined Fiji's electoral system from independence through the 1997 Constitution. All citizens vote on a single national roll regardless of ethnicity. This is one of the 2013 Constitution's most significant departures from Fiji's constitutional past. The provision is formally stable but its practical effect depends on the electoral system it operates within — and the FELRC's finding that the current open list system does not adequately serve the non-communal goals that s.53 reflects.
Universal suffrageNon-communalSingle roll
"Members of Parliament must vote in Parliament in accordance with the decision of their respective political parties on all matters before Parliament."
FPR Analysis
Section 63 compels MPs to vote with their party on every parliamentary matter — eliminating individual conscience votes and creating one of the most rigid party discipline provisions in any Commonwealth constitution. An MP who votes against their party's direction vacates their seat. Critics argue it reduces Parliament to a rubber stamp, removes individual accountability to constituents, and makes cross-party deliberation impossible. Defenders argue it creates stable government and prevents floor-crossing for personal advantage — a genuine concern in Fiji's parliamentary history. The FELRC reviewed this provision and the CRC must engage with those findings directly.
Party disciplineConscience voteParliamentary independence
CRC Watch
The Commission should consider whether s.63 is compatible with genuine parliamentary deliberation. A provision that requires MPs to vote as instructed on all matters fundamentally alters the nature of parliamentary representation — reducing elected members to delegates of party leadership rather than representatives of constituents.
"A bill is passed by Parliament when a majority of members of Parliament present and voting vote in favour of the bill."
FPR Analysis
Section 66 establishes a simple majority threshold for passing legislation — combined with the unicameral structure of Parliament and the strict party discipline of s.63, this creates a legislative system in which a government with a parliamentary majority can pass virtually any legislation it wishes with minimal constraint. The 2013 Constitution abolished the Senate that existed under the 1997 Constitution — removing a revising chamber that, whatever its limitations, provided an additional layer of legislative scrutiny. The CRC should consider whether Fiji's unicameral system adequately serves the constitutional value of democratic deliberation.
Simple majorityUnicameralNo upper houseLegislative scrutiny
CRC Watch
The Commission should consider whether restoring a bicameral parliament — or creating an equivalent revising mechanism — would better serve Fiji's constitutional values. The abolition of the Senate removed a structural check on executive-legislative power that served a function distinct from its ethnic composition.
"Subject to this Constitution, Parliament continues for four years from the date of the first sitting of Parliament after a general election."
FPR Analysis
Section 71 establishes a four-year parliamentary term. The current Parliament was first seated in January 2023 following the December 2022 election, meaning the four-year term expires in early 2027. Elections must be held no later than 90 days after dissolution, meaning the general election must occur between approximately August 2026 and February 2027. The election window is significant for the CRC's work — the Commission's report is due 31 August 2026, meaning its recommendations will be published during the height of the election campaign. This creates a direct tension between constitutional reform and electoral politics.
Four-year term2026-2027 electionCRC timeline
CRC Watch
The Commission's report is due 31 August 2026 — coinciding with the 2026-2027 election window. The Commission must be transparent about how it will prevent its constitutional reform work from being captured by electoral politics in either direction.
Chapter 5The Executive

Establishes the President, Cabinet, Prime Minister, Attorney-General and senior public service positions. The concentration of executive power is one of the CRC's core review areas.

"There is a President of the Republic of Fiji who is the Head of State and the Commander-in-Chief of the Republic of Fiji Military Forces."
FPR Analysis
Section 85 establishes the Presidency as a largely ceremonial Head of State role — unlike the executive presidency proposed in some post-coup drafts. The President is elected by Parliament rather than directly by the people, reducing the office's democratic legitimacy but also its political power. The CRC's report will be submitted to the President — making the office a central recipient of the constitutional reform recommendations before they reach Cabinet and Parliament. The President's role in the constitutional review process is therefore more significant than the office's day-to-day constitutional functions might suggest.
Head of StateCommander-in-ChiefCeremonial role
"The executive authority of the State is vested in the Cabinet."
FPR Analysis
Section 91 vests executive authority in Cabinet — creating a system in which, combined with s.63 (party discipline), a Prime Minister commanding a parliamentary majority faces few effective constitutional constraints. Regan, Kirkby and Kant (2024) describe this as creating a "winner-takes-all government system" — a structure in which electoral winners hold powers "constrained mainly by the RFMF." The concentration of executive power in Cabinet, combined with the Attorney-General's role on the Judicial Services Commission (s.109) and control of the prosecutorial process through appointment powers, creates a system of concentrated authority that the CRC must address.
Executive powerCabinetWinner-takes-allConcentration of power
CRC Watch
The Commission must address whether adequate checks exist on executive power — or whether the 2013 Constitution has created a system in which a government with a parliamentary majority faces insufficient institutional constraint. The October 2025 FICAC investigations of Cabinet ministers raise this question in acute form.
"There must be an Attorney-General who is the principal legal adviser to the Government of Fiji."
FPR Analysis
Section 95 establishes the Attorney-General as the government's principal legal adviser. The AG's significance in Fiji's constitutional architecture is magnified by two features of the 2013 Constitution: the AG sits on the Judicial Services Commission (giving executive influence over judicial appointments) and the FELRC's final report was submitted to the Acting AG in July 2025 — where it has remained for nine months without being tabled in Parliament. Whether the suppression of the FELRC report is consistent with the AG's constitutional role as the government's principal legal adviser — whose advice should include advice on constitutional compliance — is a live question.
Attorney-GeneralFELRC reportJudicial appointments
CRC Watch
The FPR has sought comment from the Acting AG on why the FELRC report has not been tabled in Parliament nine months after submission. No response has been received. The Commission should require the AG to explain this delay as part of its review process.
"There is a Director of Public Prosecutions who is responsible for instituting and conducting criminal proceedings against any person before any court."
FPR Analysis
Section 102 establishes the DPP as the independent prosecutorial authority. In practice, the independence of prosecutorial institutions in Fiji — particularly FICAC — has been questioned in several high-profile cases. The Charters case raised allegations that FICAC was used to pressure a journalist to reveal sources. The prosecutions of former Deputy Prime Ministers Prasad and Kamikamica have been both praised as evidence of anti-corruption commitment and questioned as potentially politically motivated. The DPP's relationship with FICAC — which has independent prosecution powers — creates a complex institutional landscape that the CRC should examine.

Chapter 6The Judiciary

Establishes Fiji's court system and judicial independence framework. The role of the Attorney-General in judicial appointments is a key CRC concern.

"The judiciary is independent and subject only to this Constitution and the law, which the judiciary must apply without fear, favour or prejudice."
FPR Analysis
Section 97 guarantees judicial independence — the principle is formally strong and the text is unambiguous. The Supreme Court demonstrated its independence powerfully in its August 2025 advisory opinion, reading down the constitutional amendment threshold against the government's preferred interpretation. However, judicial independence is only as strong as the appointment process that determines who sits on the bench. The Attorney-General's role on the Judicial Services Commission creates a structural tension between executive influence and judicial independence that s.97 alone cannot resolve.
Judicial independenceRule of lawSupreme Court 2025
"There is a Judicial Services Commission that is responsible for recommending persons for appointment as judges of the High Court, the Court of Appeal and the Supreme Court."
FPR Analysis
Section 109 establishes the Judicial Services Commission. The Attorney-General is a member of the JSC — meaning the government's principal legal adviser sits on the body that recommends judicial appointments. This creates a structural conflict of interest between executive and judicial independence that is difficult to reconcile with s.97's guarantee of judicial independence. The CRC should consider whether the AG's JSC membership is compatible with genuine judicial independence — or whether it represents a structural mechanism for executive influence over the composition of the courts.
Judicial appointmentsAG on JSCStructural conflict
CRC Watch
The Commission should recommend removing the Attorney-General from the Judicial Services Commission. No other comparable democratic system places the government's principal legal adviser on the body responsible for recommending judicial appointments.
Chapter 7Finance

Governs public finance, the Reserve Bank, and fiscal accountability. Relevant to debates about sovereign wealth fund architecture and fiscal discipline.

"All revenues and other moneys raised or received by the State form part of the Consolidated Fund, which is the central fund of the State."
FPR Analysis
Section 121 establishes the Consolidated Fund — the mechanism through which all State revenues flow and from which expenditures are appropriated. The provision is formally stable but its implications extend to the sovereign wealth fund debate the FPR has examined. A future Sovereign Resilience Fund would need to sit outside the Consolidated Fund's normal operation — requiring either a dedicated statutory mechanism or a constitutional provision that ring-fences accumulated savings from normal budget appropriation. The absence of any such mechanism is one reason Fiji has never built intergenerational savings — the Consolidated Fund absorbs revenues that other countries preserve in dedicated instruments.
Public financeConsolidated FundFiscal architecture
"There is an Auditor-General who is responsible for auditing and reporting on the public accounts of the State."
FPR Analysis
The Auditor-General provides independent oversight of public expenditure — one of the core accountability provisions of the Constitution. The office's practical independence depends on its resourcing, the extent to which its reports are acted upon, and whether Parliament's Public Accounts Committee functions as an effective oversight mechanism. The CRC should consider whether the Auditor-General's constitutional mandate is adequate — and whether there are mechanisms to ensure that audit findings result in accountability rather than simply being noted and set aside.

Chapter 8Accountability and Transparency

Establishes FICAC, the Human Rights Commission, and other accountability institutions. The independence of these institutions is among the most contested governance questions in Fiji's current political moment.

"There is a commission to be known as the Fiji Independent Commission Against Corruption, which is responsible for taking measures to investigate and combat corruption in Fiji."
FPR Analysis
Section 136 establishes FICAC — the body responsible for investigating and combating corruption. In the current political moment, FICAC's record is deeply contested. On one hand, it has investigated and charged senior political figures including two former Deputy Prime Ministers — evidence that it is willing to pursue powerful interests. On the other hand, critics have alleged that FICAC's powers have been misused — the Charters arrest allegedly sought to pressure a journalist to reveal sources, and the Malimali appointment controversy raised questions about whether the government attempted to interfere with FICAC's independence. The CRC should consider whether FICAC's governance structure adequately protects its independence from executive influence.
Anti-corruptionFICAC independenceCharters caseMalimali
CRC Watch
The Commission should examine whether FICAC's appointment and reporting structure adequately insulates it from executive influence — and whether the agency's powers are subject to sufficient oversight to prevent their misuse in political cases.
"There is a Human Rights Commission responsible for promoting and protecting human rights in Fiji."
FPR Analysis
Section 145 establishes the Human Rights Commission. The FPR's constitutional submission identified a significant practical limitation on the Commission's effectiveness — the Constitution originally restricted complaints to acts occurring after the Constitution's commencement in September 2013. This temporal restriction meant the Commission could not investigate human rights abuses committed during the 2006–2013 period — the years of military rule following the coup. The CRC must address whether this limitation remains appropriate and whether the Human Rights Commission has adequate resources and independence to fulfil its constitutional mandate.
Human rightsTemporal restriction2006-2013 period
CRC Watch
The Commission should remove or qualify the temporal restriction on Human Rights Commission complaints. The inability to investigate abuses committed during the military rule period is a direct constitutional protection for those responsible — and is inconsistent with the rule of law values the Constitution declares foundational.
Chapter 9Security Services

Establishes the constitutional mandate of the Republic of Fiji Military Forces — the provision that most distinguishes the 2013 Constitution from conventional democratic constitutions.

"The Republic of Fiji Military Forces exists to ensure at all times the security, defence and well-being of Fiji and all Fijians."
FPR Analysis
Section 131 gives the RFMF a constitutional mandate that extends well beyond conventional military functions. The phrase "well-being of Fiji and all Fijians" has been interpreted as granting the military an expansive supervisory role over democratic governance. This is the provision that gives the 2013 Constitution its fundamentally undemocratic character — the military is positioned not merely as subject to civilian control but as a guardian of it. Regan, Kirkby and Kant (2024) describe the RFMF as maintaining an "extra-constitutional and expansive role as protector of the Fijian people and the 2013 Constitution." No comparable democratic constitution gives its military forces a mandate over the "well-being" of its citizens.
RFMF mandateWell-being clauseExtra-constitutionalCivilian control
CRC Watch
Section 131 is the most constitutionally significant structural problem in the 2013 Constitution. The Commission must recommend that the RFMF's mandate be restricted to conventional defence and security functions — removing the "well-being" provision that provides a constitutional basis for future military intervention in democratic governance.
Chapter 10Immunity Provisions

Shields those responsible for the 2006 coup and subsequent military governance from legal accountability. These provisions are constitutionally entrenched and cannot be amended through the ordinary amendment process.

"No court or tribunal has jurisdiction to accept, hear or in any way entertain or determine any proceeding, whether civil or criminal, that challenges or calls into question directly or indirectly any act done, matter or thing done or occurring, or any decision made in the period from 5 December 2006 to the date of commencement of this Constitution."
FPR Analysis
Section 158 grants blanket immunity from legal proceedings for all acts committed between the 2006 coup and the commencement of the 2013 Constitution. It effectively immunised Frank Bainimarama and all those who acted under his authority during that period. The provision is embedded in Chapter 10, which is itself immune from the ordinary amendment process — meaning it cannot be removed by any parliamentary supermajority combined with referendum approval. It can only be changed through wholesale constitutional replacement. This makes s.158 constitutionally permanent in practical terms — the most significant unamendable provision in a document with several structural entrenchments.
Coup immunity2006-2013 periodUnamendableChapter 10
On unamendability: Section 158 sits in Chapter 10, which is specifically excluded from the constitutional amendment process. Even with a three-quarters parliamentary supermajority and referendum approval, Chapter 10 cannot be changed. The immunity provisions are constitutionally permanent unless the entire Constitution is replaced — which is itself a complex political and legal process.
CRC Watch
The Commission cannot recommend amendment of s.158 through the ordinary amendment process. The Commission must address whether to recommend a new constitution — rather than amendment of the existing one — as the only mechanism through which the immunity provisions could be reviewed.
Chapter 11Amendment of the Constitution

Governs how the Constitution may be amended. The Supreme Court's August 2025 advisory opinion fundamentally changed the amendment landscape — making constitutional reform more achievable than at any previous point since 2013.

"A bill to amend any provision of this Constitution must be supported by the votes of at least three-quarters of all the members of Parliament at its second and third readings."
FPR Analysis
Section 160 sets the amendment threshold for constitutional change. The provision originally required a three-quarters majority of all registered voters in a referendum — creating what constitutional scholars call "functional unamendability," as this threshold could not realistically be achieved. The Supreme Court's advisory opinion of 29 August 2025 (Miscellaneous Action No. 01 of 2025) read down this threshold, ruling that amendments may proceed with a two-thirds parliamentary majority followed by a simple majority of votes cast in a referendum, provided minimum turnout conditions are met. This single judicial decision fundamentally changed Fiji's constitutional landscape — making the CRC's work practically viable for the first time. However, the three-quarters parliamentary supermajority requirement at the second and third readings remains — demanding cross-party consensus that has historically been difficult to achieve in Fiji's polarised political environment.
Amendment procedureSupreme Court 2025Three-quarters majorityReferendum threshold
CRC Watch
The August 2025 advisory opinion is the legal foundation for the Commission's entire work. Without it, any recommendations requiring constitutional amendment would face an effectively insurmountable barrier. The Commission should note in its report the continuing constraint of the three-quarters parliamentary supermajority — and whether it recommends any modification to make the amendment process more workable while maintaining appropriate protection against precipitate change.
Chapter 12General and Transitional Provisions

Governs the transition from the Bainimarama administration's governance framework to the 2013 constitutional order. Includes the interpretation provisions that define how the Constitution is to be read.

"Every provision of this Constitution must be interpreted in a manner that promotes its purposes, values and principles, permits the continuous development of this Constitution and the law, and advances the rule of law and human rights."
FPR Analysis
Section 161 establishes a purposive approach to constitutional interpretation — requiring courts to interpret provisions in a manner that promotes the Constitution's underlying values rather than reading them in a purely literal or technical way. This is the interpretive framework the Supreme Court applied in its August 2025 advisory opinion when reading down the referendum threshold in s.160. The provision "permits the continuous development of this Constitution and the law" — suggesting the drafters understood that constitutional meaning would evolve through interpretation. The CRC's work must be understood within this interpretive framework.
Purposive interpretationConstitutional developmentRule of law
"Parliament must make provision for the conduct of referendums, including referendums held for the purposes of amending this Constitution."
FPR Analysis
This provision requires Parliament to legislate for referendum conduct — which the National Referendum Bill No. 46 of 2025 purports to do. The FPR's first published analysis examined the Bill in detail and found that several of its provisions — particularly those restricting public communication about constitutional amendments during the referendum campaign period — raise significant questions about whether the Bill facilitates or constrains the genuine public deliberation that a constitutional referendum requires. The CRC's recommendations will ultimately be put to the people in a referendum governed by this legislation — making the quality of the referendum framework directly relevant to whether the CRC's work achieves legitimate constitutional change.
Referendum legislationNational Referendum BillPublic deliberation
CRC Watch
The Commission should assess whether the National Referendum Bill's communication restrictions are consistent with the genuine public deliberation a constitutional referendum requires.
